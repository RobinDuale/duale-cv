Turning pressure into ambition:
is that really what makes the difference?

May 5, 2026 · 6 min read

A headhunter working on behalf of a fund put this question to me simply: "How do you manage shareholder pressure when your teams don't yet understand why the targets have been raised?" It struck me not because it was difficult, but because it asked exactly the right question. Pressure is structural. Ambition takes hold. The gap between the two can be managed.

When the fund's ambition enters the organisation

A fund does not buy what you are. It buys what you can become. This distinction is critical, and it is often the first source of friction in the months following closing. Management receives a three-year plan with growth targets the organisation has never hit before. Meanwhile, operational teams continue running on the reflexes that got them this far. The gap between the two is not a question of willingness. It is a question of framing.

The temptation for the CEO in this situation is to absorb the board's pressure in order to shield the teams. To filter, smooth over, and reassure. That is a mistake. Teams read the signals. They sense the acceleration without understanding the logic behind it. They see decisions speed up without grasping the thread connecting them. This disconnect produces a diffuse anxiety that is more corrosive than explicit pressure.

What I have learned to do is the opposite: explain the mechanics to management first, then progressively to the teams. Not to share the stress, but to share the perspective. A team member who understands why the company wants to double its ARR in eighteen months does not simply endure the target. They can take ownership of it, challenge it, and contribute with their own reading of the situation.

The boomerang effect of poorly calibrated targets

I have seen ambitious growth plans produce exactly the opposite of the intended effect. Targets set without equipping the organisation to meet them, without matching resources, without adapting processes. In these cases, pressure does not generate acceleration. It generates exhaustion, then disengagement, then departures.

The problem is not ambition. The problem is the gap between the stated ambition and the organisation's actual capacity to carry it. That gap can be measured. It can be worked on. It requires the CEO to be not just a conduit for the fund's wishes, but a translator between what the fund wants to build and what the organisation can absorb at any given moment.

I have pushed back on board-presented targets on several occasions. Not to minimise them, but to shift them in time or make them conditional on prior investment. A serious fund prefers a plan that holds over a plan that promises and then unravels. The CEO who defends this logic with data gains credibility. The one who validates without challenge loses it progressively, as soon as the first gaps appear.

Calibrating means identifying what the organisation can become

Turning pressure into ambition does not happen through motivational speeches. It happens through calibration work that starts with an honest read of the organisation: where are the current friction points, what skills are missing, which processes are slowing growth, which people are ready to step up in a more demanding environment.

I always conduct this read by bringing management into the diagnosis. Not to share the burden of a future result, but because they have the on-the-ground visibility. A sales director who has taken part in analysing the constraints facing their team does not receive a raised quota target as a top-down directive. They receive it as the conclusion of work they helped build.

Calibration produces two things. First, a credible plan: reachable milestones, allocated resources, clear indicators. Second, a form of collective ownership: the team that helped define what is realistic has a different relationship to the target. They know where it came from. They can defend it to their own teams.

Behind ambition, a vision to embody

A shareholder's pressure is always financial in the final analysis. It expresses itself in multiples, IRR, anticipated exit dates. But an organisation does not run on those metrics. It runs on a sense of what it is building and the role it plays in something larger than hitting a ratio.

The CEO in a PE context must hold both frames simultaneously. Report to the board in the board's language: ARR, NRR, EBITDA, valuation trajectory. And give teams meaning in a language of purpose and project: why this growth, for which customer, towards which product, with what five-year vision.

This is not a contradiction. It is a translation. And it is, in my view, the CEO's central job under growth pressure: not to choose between the two frames, but to articulate them with enough coherence that every level of the organisation finds its place in the shared project.

To come back to the headhunter's question: no, turning pressure into ambition is not what makes the difference. It is a prerequisite. What makes the difference is the consistency with which you return to it, at every board meeting, every leadership committee, every revised plan. This work is never done once. It is redone every time the frame shifts.
