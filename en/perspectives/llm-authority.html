Building LLM authority now:
is it a matter of survival?

May 5, 2026 · 5 min read

Surprise. In one month, my personal website is already surfacing in LLMs when people search for Robin Dualé. No backlinks, no agency, no acquisition budget — just a coherent editorial architecture built around a targeted thematic focus, developed with Claude.

This signal, however anecdotal at this scale, raises a question I now consider unavoidable for business leaders: if the next visibility battle is being fought inside LLM responses rather than on Google results pages, what is the strategic value of building thematic authority today — at a moment when this space is still largely uncontested?

A behavioral shift the data is beginning to document

Studies on the subject point in the same direction: the share of searches that generate no click to an external site is growing rapidly, driven by the emergence of AI summaries at the top of results. This phenomenon is estimated to affect around 60% of search sessions today, and even more when a conversational summary appears. Gartner forecasts a 25% decline in traditional search volume by the end of 2026, driven by the rise of AI agents and chatbots. On the usage side, under-30s look for a direct answer, and their first instinct is increasingly an LLM.

For a business leader, the challenge is to ensure the company is well positioned where opinion forms before the purchase decision — making LLM visibility an increasingly strategic issue. Available studies suggest that sources actually cited in AI responses benefit from a significant visibility boost.

A disruption slower to detect than the one in the 2000s

What I witnessed from the inside in the 2000s was a relatively clear substitution: paper catalogs were disappearing, shops without a web presence were losing customers in identifiable ways, and the warning signal was readable for anyone willing to look.

The current shift seems to follow a more subtle dynamic — an additional intermediation layer gradually inserting itself between intent and the purchase decision, without the traffic decline being immediately attributable to any specific cause.

In this context, any source that has built verifiable thematic authority on a specific topic and is named by an LLM occupies a privileged position — while the still-uncontested nature of this space makes the absence of others largely invisible for now. The problem is that the wake-up call risks being painful for those who realize this late, just as it was in the 2000s.

Why some companies surface in AI responses and others disappear

The useful question for a business leader is strategic: on what basis does an LLM decide to cite my offer, my products or my services rather than another?

An LLM retains a source because it has learned it as a coherent thematic reference, regardless of its position on any particular keyword. Sources that surface consistently share genuine editorial depth, a consistent point of view, and a structure that lets the model clearly associate an expertise with an entity.

To illustrate this: a pharmacy that has published a dozen in-depth articles on children's sun protection over two years will likely be cited by an LLM before the sector's major player whose site consists mainly of product listings — because it has built, on that narrow topic, a thematic authority the model has learned and retained, regardless of its investment in search rankings.

The underlying logic is familiar: a source that has built verifiable thematic authority on a specific topic ends up being retained, where a generalist presence dilutes. The problem is that a business leader tracking Google rankings has no visibility into what LLMs retain or ignore about their company — and the tools to measure this space are only just beginning to emerge.

Why this should be on the strategic agenda now

In a previous article, I described how I approached my own transition as a product launch — applying the same reasoning around positioning, targeting, and presence where the target is. This go-to-market logic applies to LLM visibility just as it applies to any market.

"Content is king, data is queen" — this has always been true, well before LLMs. The best SEO strategies have always relied on the same discipline: identify the territories where the company has genuine legitimacy and treat them with depth rather than spreading too thin. LLMs follow the same rule, reinforcing the premium for brands whose expertise is readable, consistent and verifiable, and making thematic dispersion even more costly.

In this context, a vertical player can become more credible than a generalist platform: Amazon will remain dominant for answering "where to buy?" — but a specialist e-commerce business can better answer "what to choose?", "for whom?" and "with what precautions?", by becoming an identifiable source on a specific decision territory.

LLM visibility remains a largely open space for SMBs and BtoC e-commerce sites today — and those who position themselves now benefit from a window that will not stay open indefinitely. When large brands have structured their editorial authority on their respective markets, businesses that haven't started will find themselves in an already-occupied space, with no guarantee of breaking through.

My intuition from all this: a company with genuine sector expertise that invests now in coherent editorial production on that specific perimeter is likely building a strategic lead that will be hard to close in eighteen months — because thematic authority takes time to build, and it cannot be bought when the need becomes urgent.

Survival may be too strong a word, and no one reads the future in a crystal ball — but at every major channel disruption over the past twenty years, the question "is this a matter of survival?" was not asked when the window was still open. It was asked when it had already closed. That is the risk that, to my mind, deserves to be taken seriously now.

Running three publishing houses — on markets where content is the product, not a communication vehicle — taught me that editorial discipline is a core competency that can be structured, targeted and measured with the same rigor as a commercial strategy. What LLMs seem to reward today follows the same logic, and the difference is that it now becomes a competitive edge for sectors that never thought of themselves as editorial players.

Key takeaways
  • LLM visibility is becoming as strategic as Google SEO, and the space to build thematic authority is still largely open for SMBs.
  • An LLM cites a source because it has learned it as a coherent reference on a specific topic: an expert SMB can outrank a much larger generalist player.
  • The issue is strategic, not technical: it is the place where opinion forms before the purchase decision that is shifting.
  • Thematic authority takes time to build and cannot be bought in urgency. The window is open today — but it will not stay open long.
