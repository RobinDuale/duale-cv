Why SaaS migrations fail
and how to fix them.

You're a PE fund. You've backed a software company doing $50M in perpetual license revenue. Your thesis says: "Move this to SaaS. Recurring revenue. Higher multiples. Predictable cash flow." Your thesis is right. Your timeline is fantasy.

I've executed two major migrations to subscription—one gradual over 13 years (WEKA), one urgent over 24 months post-acquisition (Quick-FDS). Both taught me the same lesson: SaaS migrations don't fail on pricing models. They fail on organization structure. You don't move to SaaS by changing your billing system. You move by restructuring how your company operates.

Most teams miss this. They think SaaS is a pricing problem. It's not. It's that perpetual licensing and SaaS are fundamentally different business models that require fundamentally different organizations. One is event-driven (close the deal, ship the product, move on). The other is continuous (deliver every month, forever, or until customer churn).

That difference breaks three critical parts of your organization immediately:

1. Sales: From closing deals to protecting relationships

In perpetual, a sales rep closes a $300K contract that funds three years. Close in December, celebrate, hunt the next deal. In SaaS, that same $300K is $25K per month. If your customer is frustrated at month 3, they cancel at month 4. You've lost $225K of revenue you thought was locked.

Your entire sales organization—incentives, processes, hiring, comp—is built around the deal. Hunters who are great at closing are terrible at retention. Your sales structure breaks. Your forecast breaks. Your commission plan breaks.

The fix: Split the team. One side hunts new customers. One side protects existing customers. Different incentives, different metrics, different skill sets. Trying to do both with the same team? Expensive failure.

2. Operations: From ship-once reliability to always-on availability

Perpetual model: ship product, bill customer, costs decline. SaaS model: ship product, bill month 1, then bill every month forever. No downtime windows. 3am on Sunday, your product must work. Any dip in uptime shows immediately in support tickets and churn.

Infrastructure built for shipping updates quarterly doesn't hold. You'll need cloud migration, 24/7 monitoring, incident response, automation. Your CFO will see that bill and ask if you're insane. Your engineering team will resist spending cycles on "reliability" instead of "features." But features don't matter if customers can't log in.

Real cost: infrastructure doubles. Support cost triples. You're managing continuous operations against a team trained for cyclical release sprints. The tension is permanent until you restructure operations around availability, not releases.

3. Finance: From predictable peaks to dependency on retention

Perpetual: you know October revenue for December (close those deals). Plan around it. Predictable. SaaS: you know this month's MRR. You don't know next month. That depends entirely on whether customers are happy. If churn is 15% monthly, that $100K MRR becomes $70K in six months. You have zero control.

For PE funds, this is actually great—you see the true business health monthly, not annually. For management used to managing toward year-end, it's chaos.

What actually determines success

Three things determine whether your SaaS migration succeeds:

1. Alignment on the "why." PE with a 3-4 year exit window? SaaS makes sense—higher valuation multiples, better cash flow story, more attractive to buyers. Family business focused on next-quarter cash? SaaS kills you. You destroy Q1-Q2 cash flow for theoretical long-term gains. Most organizations don't have this conversation explicitly.

2. Budget for transition separate from operations. Migration costs money: infrastructure, talent, pricing experiments, customer success rebuilds, sales retraining. If you don't budget this separately and fund it outside your normal P&L, you'll strangle the project. Successful migrations: separate 24-36 month budget, funded by investors or alternate revenue. Failed migrations: try to fund transition out of operating cash flow and die slowly.

3. Tolerance for chaos. You'll have 18-24 months where both models run. Revenue looks down (customers migrating). Costs look up (double infrastructure, double support). You'll be under massive pressure to stop. Teams under stress want to revert to known models. The companies that win are the ones that hold. The ones that don't, they revert—slightly damaged, slightly skeptical.

I haven't seen a clean migration under 18 months. The fastest? 24-30 months. That was with favorable market winds and impatient investors pushing the team hard.

Bottom line: SaaS migration isn't a pricing project. It's an operational transformation that requires restructuring sales, operations, and finance. If you're not prepared to overhaul all three, you're not ready to migrate. Don't start until you are.
